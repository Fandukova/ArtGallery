Surrealism

The Genre

Surrealism is an artistic and cultural movement that emerged in Europe, following World War I. It can be characterized by depicting uncommon imagery combined with ordinary everyday objects, in order to create extravagant and illogical scenes. Surrealists were heavily influenced by the Dada movement and by the work of Sigmund Freud, who stated in his theory of dreams and the subconscious that “insanity was the breaking of the chains of logic”. The movement officially started when French poet André Breton was in Paris and published the Surrealist manifesto on October 24, 1924. This made Paris a focal point of the movement which grabbed the attention of the whole world.


The Artist

By far the most famous surrealist was the extravagant and bizarre Spanish artist, Salvador Dalí. He often depicted a dream world where reality and surreality juxtaposed. In the background was the ever-present bleak sunlit landscape that was inspired by his Catalan homeland. By far his most famous piece of art is The Persistence of Memory (1921), which depicts melting clocks on the aforementioned landscape. The main symbols of Dalí’s art were clocks, elephants, ants, eggs, and snails. The melting clocks from 1921 symbolise time devouring itself and everything around it. The exaggerated elephants, which carry immense weight on their backs, are usually depicted with long and thin legs, and are a symbol of the future and strength. Not only was Salvador Dalí’s art impressive, but so was his charm and grandeur, which made him into a worldwide celebrity.
